Wolf on Meaning in Life

Meaning in Life vs. Meaning of Life

  • What is it that makes life, as such, meaningful? (Meaning of life)
  • What is it that makes one's life meaningful, or one life as compared to another? (Meaning in life)
  • Wolff is concerned with the second question
    • Distinguish "good life" from "moral life" and "happy life"

Meaningfulness of a Life

  • Meaningfulness as part of the conditions for living a good life


[the] idea of meaningfulness…is an idea of philosophical interest, for it is an idea of a significant way in which a life can be good, a category or dimension of value, if you will, which we have a serious reason to want for ourselves and for those we care about, and which is neither subsumable under nor reducible to either happiness or morality. (Wolf, 8)

  • The notion of a "good" life is not the notion of a moral life, but rather of a life worth living
  • Introduce notion of subjective and objective assessments of this

Subjective & Objective

  1. Do what you love (subjective)
  2. Be part of something larger than yourself (objective)
  • These are two common claims made about sources of meaning. Are they convincing on their own?

Subjectivity & The Fulfillment View

The reason one should find one’s passion and go for it, then, is because doing so will give one’s life a particular type of good feeling. (Wolf, 13)


The Fullfillment View
Do what you love because it feels good

Hedonism & Fulfillment

Hedonism
the only thing that matters is pleasure (in one form or another)


  • Life has meaning insofar as it maximizes feeling fulfilled (a kind of pleasure)

What's Wrong With Hedonism?

  1. "Sisyphus fulfilled"
  2. The experience machine
  • Two examples, the first from Wolf, the second from Nozick

Sisyphus Fulfilled

  • Would Sisyphus's life be more meaningful if he felt good while rolling the rock?


Sisyphus’s task is no longer boring—no longer boring to Sisyphus, that is. But it remains futile. There is no value to his efforts; nothing ever comes of them. Even if due to divine intervention, Sisyphus comes to enjoy and even to feel fulfilled by his activity, the pointlessness of what he is doing doesn’t change. (Wolf, 17)

  • Ask poll question whether S's life is more meaningful
  • Wouldn't S be more likely deluded or deranged?
  • If we think there is anything missing in S's life then FV is wrong in some way

The Experience Machine

Suppose there were an experience machine that would give you any experience you desired. Superduper neuropsychologists could stimulate your brain so that you would think and feel you were writing a great novel, or making a friend, or reading an interesting book. All the time you would be floating in a tank, with electrodes attached to your brain. Should you plug into this machine for life, preprogramming your life's experiences? … What else can matter to us, other than how our lives feel from the inside? (Nozick, Anarchy, State, and Utopia 43)

  • Ask poll question whether one would get in the experience machine
  • Nozick picks out two issues
    • We want to do certain things, not just have the experience of doing them
    • We want to be a certain way, not just have the experience of so being
  • Nozick considers plugging into the machine to be "a kind of suicide"

The Need for Objective Value

  • If what's wrong with Sisyphus & the participant in the experience machine is something objective then what is it?
    • Pursuit of some end that is worth doing
    • Pursuit of being a certain kind of person
  • Our two cases point to the importance of objective meaning, but hard to say what that is or how it conceits to subjective fulfillment
  • Perhaps it is something "larger than oneself"
  • Is it better to be a human unsatisfied than a pig satisfied? (Mill)

The Bipartite View

in order for a life to be meaningful both an objective and a subjective condition must be met: A meaningful life is a life that a.) the subject finds fulfilling, and b.) contributes to or connects positively with something the value of which has its source outside the subject. (Wolf, 20)

  • Where "outside the subject" is meant as "something larger than oneself"


  • What is the relation between (a) and (b) ?
    • Cannot be merely accidental
      • pot-smoker
    • Cannot be solely for something positive
      • conscripted soldier

The Source of Meaning in Life

the idea is that a person’s life can be meaningful only if she cares fairly deeply about some thing or things, only if she is gripped, excited, interested, engaged, or as I earlier put it, if she loves something—as opposed to being bored by or alienated from most or all that she does. Even a person who is so engaged, however, will not live a meaningful life if the objects or activities with which she is so occupied are worthless. (Wolf, 9)

The Fitting Fulfillment View

  • A life is meaningful to the degree that it
    • (a) is led in pursuit of things, or by engaging in activities, that are worthy
    • (b) it is in virtue of the pursuit of such things, or engagement in worthy activities, that one comes to feel a particular way (i.e. "fulfilled")


According to the conception of meaningfulness I wish to propose, meaning arises from loving objects worthy of love and engaging with them in a positive way (Wolf, 8)

  • Does the view help us find an answer to Camus's fundamental philosophical question?
